OCAP Principles in Practice: What Data Sovereignty Actually Looks Like

👤
Null&Void Analytics
Principal Consultant
February 28, 2026 · 8 min read

When First Nations organizations ask about analytics, the first conversation is never about tools, vendors, or technology. It's about who owns the data. OCAP® — Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession — isn't a compliance checkbox. It's a fundamentally different way of thinking about how analytics systems get built, and who they ultimately serve.

Why the standard analytics approach doesn't work here

Most analytics projects follow a predictable pattern: the organization identifies data requirements, a vendor builds a system, data flows from the organization's systems into the vendor's infrastructure or a third-party cloud, reports are generated, and the organization pays for access.

For First Nations organizations, every element of that pattern is a problem.

Data about community members — health records, wellness indicators, housing status, justice involvement — carries a weight of historical exploitation that goes far beyond ordinary privacy concerns. Indigenous data has been collected, used, and weaponized by governments and institutions for generations, often against the interests of the communities it describes. The right to own and control that data is not a technical preference. It's a sovereignty issue.

"The architecture follows the governance. Not the other way around."

What each OCAP principle means in practice

Ownership

The community owns the data about its members. Not the health authority. Not the federal government. Not the analytics vendor. This seems straightforward until you start designing a system — and realize it means the data cannot be stored anywhere the community doesn't control, cannot be accessed by any party the community hasn't explicitly authorized, and cannot be used for any purpose the community hasn't approved.

In practice, this shapes everything from the infrastructure decision (self-hosted vs cloud, whose Azure tenant) to the data sharing agreements (which external reports are permitted and which aren't) to the governance structure (who has the authority to approve new data uses).

Control

The Nation controls how data is collected, what it's used for, who can access it, and how it's shared. Before any technical work begins, we establish a governance framework with the Nation — typically involving the health director, the elected council, and community members who will use the system. This framework answers: What data is being collected? Who can see what? Under what circumstances can data leave the community's control? What happens if the project ends?

This governance document is not a nice-to-have. It is the design specification for the entire system. The architecture follows the governance, not the other way around.

Access

The community has the right to access its own data — and the right to determine who else does. In our systems, this means role-based access control that reflects the Nation's own governance structure, not an organizational chart from a software vendor. Community health workers see the data relevant to their work. Health directors see aggregated views. Council members see what the Nation has decided they should see. External parties — including us — see nothing by default.

Possession

Physical or digital custody of the data rests with the Nation. For communities with strong sovereignty requirements, this means hardware located on Nation land, running software the Nation owns, managed by staff the Nation employs or trains. Power BI Report Server on a server in the band office — not in a Microsoft datacenter in Ontario.

For communities more comfortable with cloud infrastructure, it still means the Nation's Azure tenant, with the Nation as the account holder, and contractual guarantees that the data never leaves Canadian soil.

What this means for how we work

Every First Nations engagement we take starts months before any data pipeline is built. We sit with the health director, the council, and the community members who will actually use the system. We ask a lot of questions and we listen carefully to the answers. We do not show up with a pre-built solution.

We also invest in capacity building. The goal of every First Nations analytics engagement we take is that when the project ends, the Nation owns the platform completely and has the internal knowledge to maintain and evolve it without us. That means training 2–3 internal staff as the primary administrators, writing documentation in plain language, and designing systems simple enough that non-technical staff can manage them.

The FNHA reporting automation we built for the Sto:lo Nation reduced their monthly reporting workload from 40 hours to 45 minutes. That's a real result. But the outcome we're most proud of is that they can now add new data elements, modify reports, and extend the system themselves — without calling us.
