How We Reduced an ED's Reporting Workload by 97%

👤
Null&Void Analytics
Principal Consultant
February 14, 2026 · 7 min read

A BC health authority came to us with a problem that will sound familiar to anyone who works in hospital operations: a four-hour manual reporting process, every day, producing outputs that were 24 hours stale by the time they reached the people who needed to act on them. Here's what we built, why it worked, and what the numbers looked like six months later.

The problem

Every morning, a data analyst at a BC Interior Health Authority spent four hours building the day's operational reports in Excel. They pulled data from multiple sources, reformatted it, cross-referenced it, and assembled a document that was sent to charge nurses, site managers, and the regional executive team.

By the time those reports reached the people who needed them, the data was already 24 hours old. And by the time anyone acted on what they read, another 24 hours had passed. An ED operating 48 hours behind on its own data isn't managing capacity — it's documenting history.

There was no visibility into intraday patient flow by acuity level. No way to see which units were approaching capacity before they hit it. No ability to detect patterns in patient arrival times that would let the staffing office act proactively. And no platform for the four-hospital regional executive team to compare performance across sites in real time.

What we built

Before any pipeline work, we completed a Privacy Impact Assessment in compliance with PHIA and FOIPPA. We mapped every data element we intended to process, assessed its sensitivity classification, designed role-based access controls, and got written sign-off from the health authority's privacy officer. This document was delivered before we wrote a single line of pipeline code. It also satisfied a procurement requirement the health authority had for any analytics system handling patient data.

The technical architecture used Azure Data Factory to pull from the health authority's EMR via HL7 FHIR every 15 minutes, feeding a Bronze/Silver/Gold medallion structure in their Azure Data Lake Gen2. The Gold layer powered a set of Power BI reports published to the health authority's existing Power BI Premium workspace — no new platforms, no new logins, no new IT approvals required.

Row-level security was applied at the Gold layer. Charge nurses see data for their unit. Site managers see their site. The regional executive team sees all four sites. The system enforces the access model the health authority told us they needed — not a generic role hierarchy from a software vendor.

"The analyst who used to spend every morning building that Excel report now uses the time to actually analyze the patterns the dashboard reveals."

The results — six months post-launch

  • Report generation: 4 hours → 0 hours. The daily report process is fully automated. Nobody builds it. It exists and it's current, always.
  • Data lag: 24 hours → 15 minutes. The Azure Data Factory pipeline runs every 15 minutes during operational hours. The dashboard always shows data that's at most 15 minutes old.
  • ED throughput improved 23% in the first quarter following deployment. Being able to see intraday capacity by unit allowed charge nurses to act on emerging pressure before it became a crisis.
  • CTAS 1 and 2 on-target performance improved from 88.1% to 94.2%. The most acute patients were being seen faster, consistently.
  • The dashboard was viewed 2,400 times per month in the first six months — by clinical staff, operational managers, and executives. These are not vanity metrics. They reflect a platform people actually find useful.
  • Zero PHIA incidents since deployment.

What made it work

The technology was not the hard part. Azure Data Factory, HL7 FHIR, Power BI — these are well-understood tools. What made this project work was the discipline of the process around the technology.

We spent the first two weeks doing nothing but talking to people. Charge nurses, site managers, the data analyst who had been building those Excel reports, the regional executive who received them, the IT team who owned the infrastructure. We asked what decisions each person made and what they needed to make those decisions better.

The PIA wasn't a box to check. It was a forcing function for having explicit conversations about data access — conversations that surface assumptions and catch problems before they become incidents.

Training was three separate sessions: one for charge nurses (focused on their unit view and the metrics most relevant to their daily decisions), one for site managers (site-level comparison and operational trends), and one for the regional executive team (cross-site performance and strategic indicators). Each session was 60 minutes. Each audience got a different version of the same platform because they're making different decisions with it.
